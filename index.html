Mercury

Mercury, the innermost planet of the solar system and the eighth in size and mass. Its closeness to the Sun and its smallness make it the most elusive of the planets visible to the unaided eye. Because its rising or setting is always within about two hours of the Sun’s, it is never observable when the sky is fully dark. Mercury is designated by the symbol ☿. Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun, at a distance of 57.91 million kilometers / 35.98 miles or 0.4 AU away. It takes sunlight 3.2 minutes to travel from the Sun to Mercury.

Venus

Venus, the second planet from the Sun and the sixth-largest in the solar system, is often called the "morning star" or the "evening star" due to its brightness and visibility during certain times of the day. Its thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide traps heat, making it the hottest planet in the solar system with surface temperatures soaring up to 880 degrees Fahrenheit (471 degrees Celsius). Venus rotates on its axis in the opposite direction to most planets, causing the Sun to rise in the west and set in the east on Venus.

Earth

Earth, our beautiful blue planet, is home to a rich tapestry of life, ranging from the depths of its oceans to the heights of its towering mountains. With a diameter of approximately 12,742 kilometers (7,918 miles) and a circumference of about 40,075 kilometers (24,901 miles), Earth is the largest of the terrestrial planets in the solar system. Its atmosphere, composed primarily of nitrogen and oxygen, provides the essential conditions for life as we know it. The planet's surface is a mosaic of continents, oceans, and polar ice caps, sculpted by the forces of erosion, tectonic activity, and weathering over billions of years. Earth's dynamic geology is evident in its ever-shifting tectonic plates, which give rise to earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, and the formation of mountain ranges.

Mars

Mars, often dubbed the "Red Planet" due to its distinctive reddish appearance, is a terrestrial world with a rich history of exploration and speculation. With a diameter of about 6,779 kilometers (4,212 miles), Mars is roughly half the size of Earth, yet its surface area is comparable to the continents of our own planet. The surface of Mars is marked by a diverse array of geological features, including vast plains, towering volcanoes, deep canyons, and impact craters. Among its most prominent landmarks is Olympus Mons, the largest volcano in the solar system, which rises nearly 22 kilometers (13.6 miles) above the Martian surface.

Jupiter

Jupiter, the mighty giant of our solar system, commands attention with its immense size and striking appearance. With a diameter of approximately 139,822 kilometers (86,881 miles), Jupiter is more than 11 times wider than Earth and boasts a mass over 300 times greater than our planet's. This immense bulk is predominantly composed of hydrogen and helium, the same elements that make up the majority of the Sun. One of the most distinguishing features of Jupiter is its dynamic atmosphere, characterized by colorful bands of clouds that encircle the planet. These bands are created by powerful jet streams and differential rotation, causing clouds of varying composition and opacity to flow at different speeds and directions. Among these bands are iconic features such as the Great Red Spot, a massive storm that has raged for centuries, and numerous smaller vortices and eddies.

Saturn

Saturn, often referred to as the "Jewel of the Solar System," is a gas giant with a diameter of approximately 116,460 kilometers (72,366 miles) and a mass over 95 times that of Earth. Its composition is predominantly hydrogen and helium, similar to Jupiter, but Saturn's lower density gives it a unique appearance and structure. The most iconic feature of Saturn is undoubtedly its spectacular ring system, which consists of thousands of individual ringlets made up of icy particles ranging in size from grains of sand to large boulders. These rings, which extend hundreds of thousands of kilometers from the planet's surface, are thought to be the remnants of a shattered moon or debris left over from the formation of the planet itself.

Uranus

Uranus, named after the ancient Greek deity of the sky, is a gas giant with a diameter of approximately 50,724 kilometers (31,518 miles) and a mass roughly 14.5 times that of Earth. Unlike the other planets, Uranus rotates on its side, with its axis of rotation tilted at an angle of about 98 degrees relative to its orbit around the Sun. This extreme axial tilt gives Uranus its distinct appearance, causing it to roll on its side as it orbits the Sun. The unique orientation of Uranus' axis has profound effects on its atmosphere and seasons. Its poles experience long periods of continuous sunlight or darkness, resulting in dramatic temperature variations and atmospheric dynamics. Uranus' atmosphere is composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, with traces of methane that give it a blue-green hue.

Neptune

Neptune, named after the Roman god of the sea, is a gas giant with a diameter of approximately 49,244 kilometers (30,598 miles) and a mass approximately 17 times that of Earth. It orbits the Sun at an average distance of about 4.5 billion kilometers (2.8 billion miles), taking nearly 165 Earth years to complete a single orbit. One of the most striking features of Neptune is its vivid blue color, which is the result of methane in its atmosphere absorbing red light and reflecting blue light back into space. The presence of methane clouds high in the atmosphere gives Neptune its characteristic hue, distinguishing it from the other gas giants in the solar system.
